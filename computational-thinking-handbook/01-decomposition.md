# Module 01: Decomposition — Breaking Big Problems into Small Pieces

## What is Decomposition?

Have you ever looked at something really big and thought, "I can NEVER do that!"?

Maybe it was a giant homework assignment. Or cleaning the whole house. Or learning a new sport. It felt too big, too hard, too scary.

Here's a secret: **nothing is too big if you break it into small pieces.**

That's what **decomposition** means. It's a long word, but the idea is simple:

> **Decomposition = taking something BIG and breaking it into SMALL parts.**

Think of it like eating a pizza. You don't shove the whole pizza in your mouth at once (well, maybe you want to, but you can't!). You eat it one slice at a time. Each slice is easy to eat. And before you know it — the whole pizza is gone!

Decomposition works the same way. Take a big problem. Cut it into slices. Solve each slice. Done!

---

## Real-Life Examples

Let's look at some things you already know and see how decomposition works.

### Example 1: Making Nasi Goreng (Fried Rice)

Imagine someone says, "Make nasi goreng!" If you've never made it before, that sounds hard. But let's break it down:

**Big task:** Make nasi goreng.

**Small pieces:**
1. Cook the rice (or use leftover rice from yesterday).
2. Chop the vegetables — carrots, green onions, garlic.
3. Crack 2 eggs into a bowl.
4. Heat oil in a pan.
5. Cook the garlic until it smells good.
6. Add the vegetables and stir.
7. Add the rice and mix it together.
8. Push the rice to the side and scramble the eggs.
9. Mix everything together.
10. Add soy sauce and a pinch of salt.
11. Serve on a plate.

See? Each small step is easy. You just do them one at a time, and suddenly you've made nasi goreng!

### Example 2: Getting Ready for School

**Big task:** Get ready for school.

**Small pieces:**
1. Wake up when the alarm rings.
2. Get out of bed.
3. Go to the bathroom — brush teeth, wash face.
4. Put on your school uniform.
5. Eat breakfast.
6. Pack your backpack — check for homework, books, pencil case.
7. Put on shoes.
8. Say goodbye and head to school.

You probably do this every day without thinking about it. But you're already doing decomposition! Your brain automatically breaks "get ready for school" into small steps.

### Example 3: Building a LEGO House

**Big task:** Build a LEGO house.

**Small pieces:**
1. Find all the LEGO pieces you need.
2. Sort them by color or size.
3. Build the floor (the base).
4. Build the walls.
5. Add the windows and door.
6. Build the roof.
7. Add details — a chimney, flowers, a little person.

If someone just dumped a pile of LEGO bricks in front of you and said "Build a house!" you'd feel overwhelmed. But when you know the steps, it's totally doable.

### Example 4: Cleaning Your Room

**Big task:** Clean your entire room.

**Small pieces:**
1. Pick up all the clothes and put them in the hamper.
2. Put all the toys back in the toy box.
3. Put books on the bookshelf.
4. Throw trash in the trash can.
5. Make the bed.
6. Sweep or vacuum the floor.

Instead of staring at a messy room feeling stuck, you pick ONE thing to do first. Then the next thing. Then the next. Small steps add up to a clean room!

---

## Why Decomposition Matters

Here are three big reasons:

### 1. Big Problems Feel Less Scary

When you break a problem into pieces, each piece is small and easy. You stop feeling overwhelmed and start feeling confident. "I can do THIS small thing!"

### 2. You Can Share the Work

When a problem is broken into pieces, different people can work on different pieces. Imagine your family is cleaning the house. Mom does the kitchen. Dad does the living room. You do your bedroom. Sister does the bathroom. Everyone does a small part, and the whole house gets clean fast!

### 3. You Can Find Mistakes Easier

If something goes wrong, you can check each small piece to find where the problem is. It's like checking each ingredient when a recipe doesn't taste right. "The rice is fine... the vegetables are fine... oh! I forgot the soy sauce!"

## Decomposition in the Real World

Decomposition isn't just for kids. Grown-ups use it every day:

- **Builders** break "build a house" into: lay the foundation, put up walls, install plumbing, add the roof, paint the walls.
- **Movie makers** break "make a movie" into: write the story, find actors, film the scenes, add music, edit everything together.
- **Party planners** break "throw a party" into: choose a date, make a guest list, send invitations, buy food, decorate, plan games.
- **Game designers** break "make a video game" into: design the characters, draw the world, write the rules, add sounds, test it for bugs.

## Activity 1: The "Break It Down" Game

**What you need:** Paper and a pencil.

**How to play:**

1. Pick a BIG task from the list below (or make up your own).
2. Write the big task at the top of your paper.
3. Break it into as many small steps as you can.
4. Number each step.
5. Check: are your steps in the right order? Does anything come first?

**Big tasks to choose from:**
- Make a peanut butter and jelly sandwich
- Brush your teeth
- Plant a flower
- Wrap a birthday present
- Draw a picture of your pet (or dream pet)
- Write a letter to a friend
- Set the table for dinner
- Pack a suitcase for vacation

**Challenge:** Try to break each step into even SMALLER steps! For example, "Brush your teeth" can be broken down into:
1. Go to the bathroom.
2. Pick up your toothbrush.
3. Open the toothpaste cap.
4. Squeeze toothpaste onto the brush.
5. Turn on the water.
6. Wet the toothbrush.
7. Brush the top teeth.
8. Brush the bottom teeth.
9. Brush your tongue.
10. Spit out the toothpaste.
11. Rinse your mouth with water.
12. Rinse the toothbrush.
13. Put the toothbrush back.

The more you break things down, the better your instructions become!

## Activity 2: Decomposition Drawing

**What you need:** Paper, pencil, colored pencils or crayons.

**How to play:**

1. Choose something to draw: a cat, a car, a robot, or a house.
2. DON'T just start drawing! First, break it down.
3. On one side of your paper, write down all the PARTS of what you're drawing.

**Example — Drawing a cat:**
- Head (circle)
- Two ears (triangles)
- Two eyes (small circles)
- Nose (tiny triangle)
- Mouth (curved line)
- Whiskers (straight lines)
- Body (oval)
- Four legs (rectangles)
- Tail (curved line)

4. Now draw each part, one at a time.
5. Put them all together, and you have a cat!

**Why this is cool:** Professional artists do this too! They break complex drawings into simple shapes first, then add details.

## Activity 3: The Sandwich Challenge

**What you need:** A partner (friend, sibling, parent).

**How to play:**

1. Pretend your partner is a robot. The robot can ONLY do exactly what you tell it to do. Nothing more. Nothing less.
2. Give the robot instructions to make a sandwich.
3. The robot follows your instructions EXACTLY. If you say "put the peanut butter on the bread" but you forgot to say "open the jar first," the robot will try to spread a closed jar on the bread!
4. See how many steps it really takes.

This is funny and a great way to learn that decomposition means being VERY specific with your steps!

---

## Activity 4: My Morning Routine Flowchart

**What you need:** A large piece of paper, pencil, colored pencils.

**How to play:**

1. Think about everything you do from the moment you wake up to the moment you leave for school.
2. Draw a box for each step.
3. Connect the boxes with arrows showing the order.
4. Decorate your flowchart with colors and pictures!

**Bonus:** Hang it on your wall and follow it tomorrow morning. Did you remember every step? Did you need to add any?

## Common Mistakes

Here are some mistakes people make with decomposition:

**Mistake 1: Making pieces too big.**
"Step 1: Cook the food. Step 2: Eat." That's not broken down enough! Each piece should be small and clear.

**Mistake 2: Forgetting a step.**
"Step 1: Get in the shower. Step 2: Dry off." Wait — you forgot to actually wash! Always double-check your steps.

**Mistake 3: Steps in the wrong order.**
"Step 1: Pour cereal in the bowl. Step 2: Get a bowl." Oops! You need the bowl FIRST. Order matters!

## Challenge: The Mega-Decomposition

Here's a big challenge for you:

**Task:** Plan a school talent show.

Break this into as many small tasks as you can. Think about:
- What needs to happen BEFORE the show?
- What needs to happen DURING the show?
- What needs to happen AFTER the show?

Try to come up with at least 15 steps! Share your answer with a friend and compare — did they think of steps you missed?
