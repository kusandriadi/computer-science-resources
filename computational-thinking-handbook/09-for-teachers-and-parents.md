# Module 09: For Teachers and Parents — A Guide for Educators

## Welcome, Educators!

Thank you for exploring Computational Thinking (CT) with the young people in your life. Whether you are a classroom teacher, a homeschool parent, a tutor, or a family member helping with learning, this guide will help you make the most of this handbook.

You don't need to be a computer scientist to teach CT. The concepts are intuitive, and many of the activities in this handbook require zero technology. Your role is to **facilitate, ask good questions, and encourage exploration**.

---

## What is Computational Thinking (Quick Recap)?

Computational Thinking is a set of problem-solving skills that helps people break down complex problems and find solutions. It consists of four core skills and two supporting skills:

**Core Skills:**
1. **Decomposition** — Breaking large problems into smaller, manageable parts.
2. **Pattern Recognition** — Identifying similarities, trends, and regularities.
3. **Abstraction** — Focusing on relevant information while ignoring unnecessary details.
4. **Algorithm Design** — Creating step-by-step procedures to solve problems.

**Supporting Skills:**
5. **Logical Thinking** — Using reasoning, conditions (if/then), and Boolean logic (and/or/not) to make decisions.
6. **Debugging** — Systematically finding, identifying, and fixing errors.

These skills are not just for programming. They apply to every subject and to daily life. Research shows that CT improves problem-solving ability, logical reasoning, and academic performance across disciplines.

## How to Teach CT Concepts to Kids

### General Principles

1. **Start with the familiar.** Always connect new concepts to things children already know. Cooking, games, sports, and daily routines are rich with CT examples.

2. **Make it physical.** Young learners benefit from hands-on, kinesthetic activities. The unplugged activities in Module 08 are designed for this.

3. **Embrace mistakes.** Debugging is a core CT skill. Create a classroom culture where mistakes are seen as learning opportunities, not failures.

4. **Encourage collaboration.** Many CT tasks work best in pairs or small groups. Children learn by explaining their thinking to others.

5. **Use think-alouds.** Model your own thinking process. Say things like: "Hmm, this is a big problem. Let me break it into smaller pieces. First, I'll..."

6. **Let them struggle (a little).** Productive struggle builds resilience. Don't give answers too quickly. Ask guiding questions instead.

7. **Celebrate the process, not just the answer.** Praise clear thinking, good questions, and creative approaches — not just correct results.

### Age-Appropriate Approaches

**Ages 6-8 (Early Elementary):**
- Focus on decomposition (breaking tasks into steps) and simple pattern recognition.
- Use lots of visual and physical activities: sorting objects, drawing, movement games.
- Keep activities short (10-15 minutes).
- Use stories and characters to make concepts come alive.
- Use simple vocabulary: "break it down," "find the pattern," "what matters most?"

**Ages 9-10 (Mid Elementary):**
- Introduce all four core skills explicitly.
- Start using the terms: decomposition, pattern recognition, abstraction, algorithm.
- Activities can be longer (15-25 minutes).
- Begin introducing logical thinking with if/then scenarios.
- Encourage children to write their own algorithms.

**Ages 11-12 (Upper Elementary):**
- All six skills can be taught and practiced.
- Children can handle more complex problems and multi-step activities.
- Introduce simple pseudocode.
- Encourage students to create their own activities and games.
- Connect CT to subjects they're studying (see integration ideas below).

## Discussion Prompts for Each Module

Use these prompts to spark meaningful conversations before, during, or after each module.

### Module 01: Decomposition

- "Can you think of something that seems really hard? How could we break it into smaller pieces?"
- "What's the hardest part of getting ready in the morning? Could we break it into steps to make it easier?"
- "If you had to teach someone how to brush their teeth, and they had never done it before, what would you say?"
- "Why do you think breaking things down makes them easier?"
- "Can a problem be broken down too much? When do you have too many steps?"

### Module 02: Pattern Recognition

- "What patterns do you see in this room right now?"
- "What patterns do you follow every day?"
- "How do patterns help you learn new things?"
- "Can you think of a time when you predicted something because you noticed a pattern?"
- "Are there patterns in music? In art? In math? In stories?"

### Module 03: Abstraction

- "If you had to describe our school to someone who has never seen it, in just one sentence, what would you say?"
- "What details would you include on a map of our classroom? What would you leave out?"
- "When is it helpful to give lots of details? When is it helpful to keep things simple?"
- "How is a stick figure an example of abstraction?"
- "Can you think of a time when TOO much information made something harder?"

### Module 04: Algorithm Design

- "What's the difference between a good set of instructions and a bad set of instructions?"
- "Have you ever followed instructions that were confusing? What made them confusing?"
- "Why does the order of steps matter?"
- "Can you think of something you do every day that follows the same steps every time?"
- "What happens if an algorithm is missing a step?"

### Module 05: Logical Thinking

- "Can you think of an 'if-then' rule you follow at home or at school?"
- "How do you make decisions? What questions do you ask yourself?"
- "What's the difference between AND and OR when making decisions?"
- "How do you decide what to wear in the morning? What factors do you consider?"
- "Can you think of a game that uses logical thinking?"

### Module 06: Debugging

- "Can you think of a time when something didn't go as planned? What did you do?"
- "Why is it important to check your work step by step when something goes wrong?"
- "Is making a mistake a bad thing? Why or why not?"
- "How do you feel when you find and fix a mistake? How is that different from how you feel when you first discover the mistake?"
- "What's the difference between giving up and debugging?"

### Module 07: Everyday CT

- "Can you think of a problem you solved this week? Which CT skills did you use?"
- "How could you use decomposition and algorithms to plan your weekend?"
- "Do you think adults use Computational Thinking? Can you give an example?"

## Assessment Ideas

Traditional tests are not the best way to assess Computational Thinking. CT is about process and problem-solving, not memorizing facts. Here are more effective approaches:

### Observation

Watch students during activities and note:
- Can they break a problem into smaller parts without prompting?
- Do they notice patterns independently?
- Can they distinguish important information from unimportant details?
- Can they write clear, ordered instructions?
- Do they approach mistakes calmly and systematically?
- Can they explain their thinking to others?

**Tip:** Keep a simple checklist with each student's name and the six CT skills. Mark when you observe a child demonstrating a skill.

### Portfolios

Have students collect their best work throughout the handbook:
- Their "Break It Down" lists from Module 01
- Patterns they discovered in Module 02
- Their school map from Module 03
- Algorithms they wrote in Module 04
- Decision trees from Module 05
- Bugs they found and fixed in Module 06
- Their birthday party plan from Module 07
- Any activities from Module 08

At the end, students can review their portfolio and reflect on what they learned.

### Self-Reflection

Have students answer questions like:
- "Which CT skill do I use the most?"
- "Which CT skill is hardest for me? Why?"
- "Can I give an example of when I used CT outside of class?"
- "What would I tell a friend about Computational Thinking?"

### Project-Based Assessment

Give students a real-world problem and ask them to solve it using CT skills. Evaluate their process, not just their solution:

**Example projects:**
- "Plan a class field trip. Show us your decomposition, algorithm, and backup plan."
- "Design a new game for recess. Write the rules (algorithm), test it, and debug any problems."
- "Organize the classroom library. Use decomposition, patterns, and abstraction."
- "Create a how-to guide for something you know how to do well."

### Peer Assessment

Have students work in pairs and give each other feedback:
- "Were the instructions clear enough to follow?"
- "Did you notice any missing steps?"
- "Could you follow the algorithm without asking any questions?"

## Recommended Tools and Resources

### Unplugged Resources (No Computer Needed)

- **CS Unplugged (csunplugged.org)** — A collection of free activities that teach computer science concepts without computers. Many of the activities in Module 08 are inspired by this resource.
- **Bebras Challenge** — An international computational thinking challenge for students. Great practice problems available online.
- **This Handbook** — Use it as-is or adapt the activities for your classroom.

### Digital Tools (When Ready for Computers)

- **Scratch (scratch.mit.edu)** — A free, block-based programming language created by MIT. Perfect for ages 8-12. Children can create their own stories, games, and animations.
- **Code.org** — Free courses and activities for all ages. Includes both unplugged and digital activities. The "Hour of Code" is a great starting point.
- **ScratchJr** — A simplified version of Scratch for ages 5-7. Available as a tablet app.
- **Blockly Games (blockly.games)** — Free puzzle-based games that teach programming concepts.
- **Tynker** — A coding platform for kids with game-based learning.
- **Bee-Bot / Blue-Bot** — Programmable floor robots for young children. Great for teaching algorithms and sequencing.

### Books for Kids

- *Hello Ruby: Adventures in Coding* by Linda Liukas — A storybook that introduces coding concepts.
- *How to Be Good at Science, Technology, and Engineering* (DK) — Visual encyclopedia covering CS concepts.
- *Lift-the-Flap Computers and Coding* (Usborne) — Interactive book for younger readers.
- *Girls Who Code: Learn to Code and Change the World* by Reshma Saujani — Inspiring introduction to coding.
- *Computational Fairy Tales* by Jeremy Kubica — CS concepts taught through fairy tale stories. Covers algorithms, data structures, search, and sorting in an engaging narrative.
- *Lauren Ipsum: A Story About Computer Science and Other Impossible Things* by Carlos Bueno — A girl named Lauren falls into a strange world and solves problems using CS concepts. Great for ages 8-12.
- *Secret Coders* by Gene Luen Yang (graphic novel series, 6 books) — Kids learn coding and logic through an engaging graphic novel mystery. Visual and fun. Ages 8-12.
- *How to Code a Sandcastle* by Josh Funk — Picture book about a girl who uses coding concepts to build a sandcastle. Perfect for ages 5-8.
- *Ada Lovelace, Poet of Science* by Diane Stanley — Picture book biography of the first computer programmer. Inspires interest in computing. Ages 6-10.

### Books for Educators

- *Computational Thinking and Coding for Every Student* by Jane Krauss and Kiki Prottsman
- *Computer Science Unplugged* by Tim Bell, Ian H. Witten, and Mike Fellows (free online)
- *Teaching Computational Thinking* by Maureen D. Neumann
- *Mindstorms: Children, Computers, and Powerful Ideas* by Seymour Papert (1980, reissued) — The classic that started it all. Papert's vision of children as builders, not just consumers. Essential reading for understanding why CT matters.
- *Computational Thinking: A Beginner's Guide to Problem-Solving and Programming* by Karl Beecher — Practical guide bridging CT concepts to programming. Good for educators planning to extend into coding.
- *Learner-Centered Design of Computing Education* by Mark Guzdial — Research-backed approaches to teaching computing. Covers how people actually learn CS concepts.
- *Computer Science K-12: Imagining the Possibilities!* edited by Shuchi Grover — Collection of essays and ideas for teaching CS in K-12. Practical classroom strategies.

## Integrating CT into Other Subjects

One of the most powerful things about Computational Thinking is that it is not a separate subject — it enhances every subject. Here are ideas for integration:

### Math

- **Decomposition:** Break multi-step word problems into smaller parts. "What do we know? What do we need to find? What steps do we take?"
- **Pattern Recognition:** Find patterns in multiplication tables, number sequences, and geometric shapes. Use patterns to predict.
- **Abstraction:** Focus on the mathematical operation needed and ignore irrelevant information in word problems. "The problem mentions that Sara is wearing a red hat, but that doesn't matter for solving the math."
- **Algorithm Design:** Write step-by-step methods for addition with carrying, long division, or converting fractions. "What's the algorithm for adding two-digit numbers?"
- **Logical Thinking:** Use if/then reasoning in geometry. "IF a shape has 4 equal sides AND 4 right angles, THEN it's a square."
- **Debugging:** Check answers by working backward. Find and fix errors in calculations.

### Science

- **Decomposition:** Break the scientific method into steps: observe, question, hypothesize, experiment, analyze, conclude.
- **Pattern Recognition:** Identify patterns in data from experiments. Notice patterns in nature (life cycles, weather patterns, food chains).
- **Abstraction:** Create simple models of complex systems (a diagram of the water cycle instead of modeling every water molecule).
- **Algorithm Design:** Write procedures for experiments that others can replicate.
- **Logical Thinking:** Form hypotheses using if/then thinking. "IF plants need sunlight, THEN a plant in a dark closet will not grow well."
- **Debugging:** When an experiment doesn't produce expected results, debug: "What variable might we have missed?"

### Language Arts / English

- **Decomposition:** Break the writing process into steps: brainstorm, outline, draft, revise, edit, publish.
- **Pattern Recognition:** Identify story patterns (beginning, middle, end), rhyme patterns in poetry, spelling patterns.
- **Abstraction:** Write summaries — capture the main idea and leave out minor details. Distinguish between main ideas and supporting details.
- **Algorithm Design:** Create step-by-step guides for writing tasks. Write "how-to" texts.
- **Logical Thinking:** Analyze cause and effect in stories. Use evidence to support opinions.
- **Debugging:** Edit and proofread writing — find and fix grammar, spelling, and logic errors.

### Art

- **Decomposition:** Break a complex drawing into simple shapes. Plan art projects step by step.
- **Pattern Recognition:** Study patterns in art (tessellations, symmetry, color patterns). Create pattern-based artwork.
- **Abstraction:** Create abstract art by simplifying real objects to their essential shapes and colors. Study how artists choose what details to include.
- **Algorithm Design:** Write step-by-step instructions for an art technique (how to mix colors, how to do paper folding).
- **Debugging:** Identify what isn't working in a piece of art and adjust.

### Physical Education / Sports

- **Decomposition:** Break sports skills into smaller movements (a basketball free throw = feet placement, grip, aim, release, follow-through).
- **Pattern Recognition:** Notice patterns in opponents' play. Recognize scoring patterns.
- **Abstraction:** Focus on the key elements of a play, not every tiny detail.
- **Algorithm Design:** Create game strategies and plays as step-by-step plans.
- **Logical Thinking:** Make decisions during games. "IF the defender goes left, THEN I go right."
- **Debugging:** Analyze what went wrong after a game. Adjust strategies.

### Social Studies

- **Decomposition:** Break historical events into causes, events, and effects.
- **Pattern Recognition:** Notice patterns in history (causes of conflicts, patterns of migration).
- **Abstraction:** Create simplified maps. Summarize complex events.
- **Algorithm Design:** Create timelines as sequential algorithms of historical events.
- **Logical Thinking:** Analyze cause and effect in social systems.

## Lesson Plan Template

Here's a simple template you can use for any CT lesson:

### Lesson: [Title]

**Duration:** [Time]

**CT Skill(s):** [Which skills are the focus?]

**Materials:** [What do you need?]

**Learning Objectives:** Students will be able to...
- [Objective 1]
- [Objective 2]

**Warm-Up (5 minutes):**
- Quick review of previous concept or a fun brain teaser.
- Discussion prompt related to today's topic.

**Introduction (10 minutes):**
- Introduce the CT skill using a real-life analogy.
- Show a simple example together as a class.
- Check for understanding: "Can someone explain this in their own words?"

**Activity (15-20 minutes):**
- Hands-on activity from the handbook.
- Students work individually, in pairs, or in small groups.
- Teacher circulates, asks guiding questions, and takes observation notes.

**Sharing and Discussion (5-10 minutes):**
- Groups share their work.
- Class discusses what went well and what was challenging.
- Connect the activity to the CT skill.

**Wrap-Up (5 minutes):**
- Quick review: "What did we learn today?"
- Preview of next lesson.
- Optional: journal entry or exit ticket.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### "I'm not a technology person. Can I still teach this?"

Absolutely! Computational Thinking is about THINKING, not technology. Most activities in this handbook are unplugged. If you can follow a recipe, organize a closet, or play a board game, you already understand CT.

### "How much time should I spend on each module?"

Each module can be covered in 1-3 sessions of 30-45 minutes each, depending on the age of the students and how many activities you include. Don't rush — it's better to spend extra time and ensure understanding than to race through.

### "What if a child struggles with a concept?"

- Try a different analogy or example.
- Use a more hands-on activity.
- Pair them with a peer who understands the concept.
- Break the concept down even further (use decomposition on decomposition!).
- Come back to it later — sometimes concepts need time to sink in.

### "What if kids already know this stuff?"

Challenge them! Have them:
- Create their own activities and teach others.
- Tackle more complex problems.
- Combine multiple CT skills in a single project.
- Explore Scratch or Code.org for digital challenges.

### "How does CT connect to coding/programming?"

CT is the foundation. Programming is one application of CT. You can think of it like this:
- CT = the thinking skills (like learning to think logically and plan)
- Programming = using those skills to tell a computer what to do

You don't need to teach programming to teach CT, but CT makes learning programming MUCH easier when kids are ready for it.

### "How do I know if my teaching is working?"

Look for these signs:
- Students start breaking down problems without being prompted.
- Students use CT vocabulary naturally ("Let me break this down..." or "I see a pattern!").
- Students approach mistakes calmly and try to find the error instead of giving up.
- Students can explain their reasoning to others.
- Students transfer CT skills to other subjects and situations.
